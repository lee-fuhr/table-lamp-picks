How to choose a table lamps

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

To choose a table lamp for your bedside, focus on brightness (lumens, not watts), color temperature (warm for relaxing, cool for reading), size that fits your nightstand without crowding, and whether you want touch control for convenience. Touch lamps offer easy on/off but can be finicky with certain bulbs or static; weigh the trade-offs against a standard switch or smart bulb.

Why brightness and color temperature matter for a bedside lamp

The brightness you need depends on what you do at the bedside. For reading, aim for something in the range of a few hundred lumens, enough to see text clearly without harsh glare. For winding down, a much dimmer, warmer glow (around 2700 to 3000 kelvin) helps signal your body that it’s time to sleep. Many modern lamps are dimmable, either via a built-in knob or a compatible smart bulb, which gives you the best of both worlds. Color temperature is often overlooked but hugely affects mood and sleep hygiene. Cool white light (4000K and above) mimics daylight and can keep you alert; warm white (2700K) feels cozy and promotes relaxation. A lamp that lets you adjust color temperature or works with a tunable bulb is a versatile choice, though it often costs more. If you stick to a fixed bulb, warm is usually the safer bet for a bedside table.

How to choose the right size and shade for your nightstand

Proportion matters more than you might think. A lamp that’s too tall will shine directly in your eyes when you’re lying down; too short and it won’t cast light where you need it. A good rule of thumb is that the bottom of the lampshade should be at eye level when you’re sitting up in bed. Typical bedside lamps range from about 20 to 30 inches in total height, but that depends on your bed height and nightstand surface. The shade style also changes the quality of light. A wide, open-top shade casts light upward for ambient glow; a narrower, darker shade directs light downward for focused reading. Opaque shades hide the bulb but can create harsh shadows if not designed well. Translucent shades soften the light nicely but may let a bright bulb overwhelm a small room. Consider what fraction of the light you want to bounce off walls versus land on your book.

Should you buy a touch control lamp? Here are the real pros and cons

Touch control lamps are undeniably convenient, no fumbling for a switch in the dark, just tap the base or stem to cycle brightness levels. Many have multiple brightness settings (often three taps for low, medium, high) and a long press for off. That’s great for folks who keep the lamp in a hard-to-reach spot or have limited hand mobility. But they aren’t perfect. Touch sensitivity can be affected by humidity, static electricity, or even a pet brushing against the base. Some lamps need a specific type of bulb (dimmable CFL or LED) to work correctly; using a non-dimmable bulb can cause flickering or failure. And if the touch sensor fails, replacing it usually means buying a whole new lamp. They also tend to cost a bit more than a basic switched lamp. If you’re after convenience and don’t mind the quirks, a touch lamp is a fine choice; if you prefer absolute reliability, stick with a classic twist-knob or pull-chain.

What bulb type works best for bedside reading?

LED bulbs are the clear winner for bedside use: they run cool, last for years, and come in every color temperature you can imagine. A dimmable LED bulb (labeled “dimmable” on the package) lets you lower the light without buzzing or flickering. Incandescent bulbs give a warm, flattering light but generate heat and waste energy; they’re also getting harder to find. CFLs are cheaper but can be slow to reach full brightness and contain mercury, which is a hassle to dispose of. If you have a touch control lamp, double-check that the bulb you buy is compatible. Many touch lamps require a “dimmable” bulb to function properly, and some LED bulbs can interfere with the touch sensor due to their internal electronics. When in doubt, the lamp’s manual or product page will list compatible bulb types. For a reading lamp, a 60-watt equivalent LED (around 800 lumens) at warm white is a solid baseline.

Common extra features to consider: USB ports, adjustable arms, and more

Modern bedside lamps often include a built-in USB port for charging your phone or tablet. That’s genuinely useful if you’re short on outlets, but check the output: a 1-amp port is fine for overnight charging; a 2.4-amp port charges faster. Some lamps even have wireless charging pads built into the base. Keep in mind that adding electronics raises the price and means one more point of failure. Adjustable arms or gooseneck designs let you direct light exactly where you need it, ideal for reading in bed without disturbing a partner. Articulating lamps (like architect-style lamps) take up more space but offer the most flexibility. Swing-arm lamps mount to the wall and save valuable nightstand real estate. Think about your room layout: a lamp that has to sit on a small shelf may not fit a bulky base, while a lamp with a wide shade may crowd a narrow nightstand.

Frequently asked questions

Are touch control lamps safe to leave on overnight?

Yes, most touch control lamps are safe to leave on overnight as long as you use an LED bulb that doesn’t get hot. The touch sensor itself uses very little power. Just be sure the lamp is UL or ETL listed for safety, and avoid using a high-wattage incandescent bulb that could overheat a small shade.

Can I use a smart bulb in a touch control lamp?

Usually not well. Touch control lamps work by detecting capacitance changes in the metal base; a smart bulb’s internal electronics can interfere with that detection, causing erratic behavior. If you want smart features like voice control or scheduling, it’s simpler to buy a regular lamp with a standard switch and use a smart bulb or smart plug instead.

How do I clean a touch control lamp without damaging the sensor?

Unplug the lamp first. Use a soft, slightly damp cloth to wipe the base and stem, avoiding moisture around the sensor contacts. Never spray cleaner directly onto the lamp. For the shade, dust with a microfiber cloth or use a lint roller. If the touch sensor becomes unresponsive, try cleaning the metal surfaces gently with a dry cloth.

What height should a bedside table lamp be?

The ideal height puts the bottom of the lampshade at around eye level when you’re sitting up in bed. That typically means the lamp’s total height (base to top of shade) should be between 20 and 30 inches, but it depends on your bed height and nightstand. If the shade is too low, the light will be in your eyes; too high and it won’t reach your book.

Do touch lamps work with any bulb?

No. Touch control lamps often require a dimmable bulb to function correctly. Using a non-dimmable LED or CFL can cause the lamp to flicker, only work on one brightness level, or not turn on at all. Always check the lamp’s label or manual for compatible bulb types before buying bulbs.

What is the best color temperature for reading in bed?

For focused reading, a neutral white around 3000K to 3500K offers a good balance, bright enough to see text clearly without being too harsh. For relaxing before sleep, a warmer temperature of 2700K is better because it minimizes blue light exposure, which can interfere with your sleep cycle. Many dimmable LED bulbs let you adjust between these ranges.

In shortChoosing a bedside lamp comes down to how you’ll actually use it: for reading, pair an adjustable or directional lamp with a warm, dimmable LED; for ambiance, prioritize a soft shade and touch control if you like the convenience. Don’t overlook bulb compatibility with touch sensors, and always measure your nightstand space before buying. A well-chosen lamp makes the bedside both practical and comfortable.
